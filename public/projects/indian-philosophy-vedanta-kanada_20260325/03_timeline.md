# Indian Philosophy — Timeline

3,500 years of continuous inquiry — from the first Vedic hymns to modern neo-Vedanta. This is the story of how Indian philosophy evolved, era by era, thinker by thinker.

## Prose Narrative — Connecting the Eras

### Era 1: The Vedic Age (1500–600 BCE) — The Dawn of the System

Picture a vast, semi-nomadic civilisation spread across the Punjab plains, with no books, no classrooms, only memory. Priest-poets called **Rishis** (literally "seers") composed hymns of extraordinary literary beauty addressed to natural forces understood as deities: Indra (storm/war), Agni (fire), Varuna (cosmic order), Surya (sun), Soma (the moon-plant elixir). These hymns weren't just religious poetry — they contained sophisticated cosmological speculation.

The famous **Nasadiya Sukta** (Rig Veda 10.129) asks: "Who knows, and who can say, whence it all came, and how creation happened? The gods themselves are later than creation's day — who knows then whence it first came into being?" This is not blind faith — it's honest philosophical uncertainty, the same spirit that drives science.

The transmission technology was remarkable: 10,000+ verses memorised with zero error tolerance. Multiple recitation patterns were used to cross-check — the equivalent of checksums on an oral hard drive.

### Era 2: The Upanishadic Age (800–200 BCE) — The Great Turning

Around 800–600 BCE, something shifted. The focus moved from ritual performance to inner inquiry. Teachers began gathering students in forests (*aranya* — hence *Aranyakas*, "forest texts"), sitting near fire, asking: "What *is* this fire that we worship? What *is* the Self that perceives it? What is the relationship between the two?"

The Upanishads are the recorded conversations from this turning. They are strikingly modern in their method: hypotheses proposed, counter-hypotheses raised, experiments described, conclusions drawn — or left open. The great teacher **Yajnavalkya** is the dominant figure: bold, often blunt, willing to say "I don't know the exact form, but I know this much."

Meanwhile — this is critical — **Siddhartha Gautama** became the Buddha (c. 525 BCE) and **Mahavira** consolidated Jainism. This period saw the single greatest flowering of philosophical diversity in human history: the Axial Age. The same century produced Socrates in Greece, Confucius in China, Zoroaster in Persia, and the Buddha in India. Something in human civilisation was ready for the question "why do we suffer, and how do we get free?"

### Era 3: The Classical Darshana Age (600 BCE–800 CE) — Systematisation

The creative explosion of the Upanishadic age needed systematising. Over the next millennium, every school of thought committed its ideas to **Sutras** (aphoristic texts — a sutra is literally "thread"). Kanada wrote the Vaisheshika Sutras. Gautama wrote the Nyaya Sutras. Patanjali wrote the Yoga Sutras. Jaimini wrote the Mimamsa Sutras. Badarayana wrote the Brahma Sutras.

A sutra is extraordinarily compact — sometimes just a few syllables — designed to be memorised, with full meaning unlocked only through a commentary (*bhashya*). This is like compressed binary code that needs a decompressor to be human-readable.

Buddhism simultaneously became a vast philosophical tradition. **Nagarjuna** (2nd century CE) developed Madhyamaka philosophy — the most technically sophisticated challenge to Brahmanical metaphysics ever written. **Vasubandhu** (4th century CE) developed Yogacara — a consciousness-only philosophy that sounds, in places, remarkably like Advaita Vedanta. The cross-pollination between Buddhist and Hindu philosophy in this era is enormous.

### Era 4: The Medieval Vedanta Age (800–1400 CE) — The Three Giants from South India

Into this complex landscape stepped three philosopher-saints, all from South India, across five centuries. **Shankaracharya** from Kerala (788 CE) wrote the definitive Advaita synthesis — 32 years, enough to transform a civilisation. His method: produce commentaries on the Upanishads, the Bhagavad Gita, and the Brahma Sutras that demonstrate, rigorously, that only one non-dual consciousness is real. He walked from Kerala to Kashmir to Assam to Gujarat, debating everyone.

**Ramanuja** from Tamil Nadu (1017 CE) built on the Tamil Vaishnava devotional tradition of the **Alvars** and created Vishishtadvaita — a philosophy that preserved the reality of God, world, and souls while maintaining their essential unity. His *Sri Bhashya* directly refuted Shankara point by point.

**Madhva** from Karnataka (1238 CE) went further: he rejected any form of identity between soul and God, insisted on five eternal distinctions, and introduced a doctrine of **predestination** (*Svabhava*) — some souls are eternally destined for liberation, some for eternal suffering, some for eternal existence in a middle state. This made Dvaita the most theologically controversial of the three.

Simultaneously, the **Bhakti movement** swept across India — poet-saints writing in vernacular languages (not Sanskrit), accessible to everyone regardless of caste or gender. This democratisation of spiritual knowledge is one of medieval India's most revolutionary achievements.

### Era 5: The Modern Period (1800–present) — Global Dialogue

European colonialism forced Indian philosophy into dialogue with Western thought. **Ramakrishna Paramahamsa** (1836–1886) demonstrated in his own life that all religious paths lead to the same truth. His disciple **Swami Vivekananda** took Vedanta to the world stage — his 1893 Chicago speech on universal tolerance is still quoted globally.

**Sri Aurobindo** developed **Integral Yoga** — a synthesis of evolution theory and Vedanta, arguing that consciousness is not just the source of the world but its *goal* — evolution is Brahman progressively self-manifesting in matter.

**Ramana Maharshi** in Tiruvannamalai, Tamil Nadu, revived the simplest Advaita practice: the question *"Who am I?"* — sit with that question, trace the "I-feeling" to its source, and what you find at the source is not a person but pure consciousness.

Today, Indian philosophy is studied in universities worldwide, influences cognitive science (theories of consciousness), physics (holism vs. reductionism), and psychology (meditation research, mindfulness). The 3,500-year conversation continues.
