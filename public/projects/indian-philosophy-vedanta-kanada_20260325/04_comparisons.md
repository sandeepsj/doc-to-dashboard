# Indian Philosophy — Comparisons

Six major schools, six key questions, one tradition — but radically different answers. This file puts them side-by-side so you can see exactly where they agree and where they diverge.

## The Three Vedanta Schools — Detailed Comparison

This is where the real action is. All three use the same texts. All three are South Indian. All three are responding to each other.

| Question | Advaita | Vishishtadvaita | Dvaita |
|----------|---------|-----------------|--------|
| **Is Brahman = Atman?** | Yes, absolutely identical | No — soul is body of God, not identical | No — eternally distinct |
| **Is the world real?** | Mithya (ultimately unreal, conventionally real) | Yes, real as God's body | Yes, completely real |
| **Can a soul become God?** | Yes — realising the identity is Moksha | No — but souls eternally enjoy God's grace | No — the distinction is eternal |
| **Role of Devotion** | Lower path (for those not ready for Jnana) | Central and supreme | Supreme — only divine grace liberates |
| **Interpretation of "Tat Tvam Asi"** | Identity: "You ARE Brahman" | Relation: "You belong to Brahman as body to soul" | Difference-within-relation: "You are Brahman's servant" |
| **On Maya** | Real as cosmic power; unreal as ultimate substance | Creative power of God, not illusion | Not illusion; world is genuinely real |
| **Key scripture** | Mandukya Upanishad + Vivekachudamani | Sri Bhashya, Gita Bhashya | Brahmasutra Bhashya, Mahabharata Tatparya Nirnaya |
| **South Indian home** | Kerala + Karnataka (Sringeri) | Tamil Nadu (Srirangam) | Karnataka (Udupi) |

## Prose Analysis

### The Fundamental Disagreement

The debate between Advaita, Vishishtadvaita, and Dvaita comes down to one sentence: *"Tat Tvam Asi"* — "That Thou Art." All three agree this sentence is one of the most important in the Upanishads. They disagree completely on what it means.

Shankara reads it as **absolute identity**: the "that" (Brahman) and the "thou" (Atman) are numerically one. There is ultimately only one consciousness. This is the most radical philosophical position possible — more radical than any Western philosophy until certain readings of Spinoza.

Ramanuja reads it as **inseparable relation**: "Thou" are part of "That" the way a limb is part of a body. Soul and God are inseparable but not identical. This position is philosophically more moderate and devotionally more satisfying — you can still pray, love, and serve.

Madhva reads it as a **relation of dependence and distinction**: even the syntax can be read differently — "tvam" (thou) can mean "thy essence is dependent on That." The soul exists *by virtue of* God but is never God. This is the most theistic and in some ways most Western-compatible reading.

### Why the Difference Matters Practically

If Advaita is right, then the ultimate practice is meditation and self-inquiry — tracing the "I" to its source and dissolving the sense of separation. Prayer and ritual are useful but preliminary.

If Vishishtadvaita is right, then devotion is not a stepping stone but the destination itself. Love of God is the highest state. Returning to Vaikuntha and eternally experiencing God's presence is the goal.

If Dvaita is right, then you can never *be* God, and you should never *try* to be. The goal is perfect humility, devotion, and dependence on God's grace. Human effort has limits; grace is required.

All three traditions produced beautiful devotional poetry, profound philosophical texts, and generations of realised teachers. The fact that three such different conclusions can be drawn from the same texts is itself one of Indian philosophy's most fascinating features.

### Vaisheshika vs. Modern Science

Kanada's atomic theory is worth a special note. He proposed **Anu** (atoms) as the irreducible building blocks of matter around 600 BCE — roughly the same time as Democritus in Greece (though with no evidence of mutual influence). Where Democritus' atoms are purely mechanical, Kanada's system is more sophisticated: he explains qualities, universals, and inference alongside the atoms. Modern physics confirms the general atomist intuition, though the actual physics is very different (quantum fields rather than classical atoms).
