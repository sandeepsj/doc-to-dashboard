## 25 Most Important Facts

1. India has **1.37 million Anganwadi Centers (AWCs)** serving approximately **80 million children** under age 6 — the world's largest ECCE delivery system by enrollment. However, the Gross Enrollment Ratio for the foundational stage (ages 3–8) is only **41.4%** as of 2024–25.

2. Anganwadi Workers (AWWs) — who constitute the primary ECCE workforce — require a **minimum 10th-pass qualification** and **6 months of NIPCCD training**, far below international ECCE teacher education standards.

3. AWWs receive an **honorarium, not a salary**, ranging from **Rs 4,500 to Rs 10,000/month** depending on state — approximately **18–25% of what a government primary school teacher earns**.

4. Annual AWW turnover is **15–20% nationally**, with Rajasthan, Uttar Pradesh, and Bihar showing the highest rates due to lowest honoraria and poorest working conditions.

5. **Das et al. (2013)** surveyed teachers in Delhi and found that approximately **70% had received no special education training**, and **87% lacked any classroom support services** for children with disabilities.

6. **Three in four children with disabilities under age 5** do not attend any educational institution in India — the earliest and highest-impact window for intervention is precisely where access is most denied (UNICEF ROSA/ERIC data).

7. India has approximately **35,000 trained special educators** (RCI data 2022) against an estimated **8 million children with disabilities** aged 0–6 — a supply-to-need ratio of approximately **1:229**.

8. Only **10% of children with disabilities in India** receive any form of early intervention services, despite the neuroplasticity window of 0–3 years being the highest-impact intervention period.

9. A **disability-specific module** (2 days) was added to the NIPCCD AWW 6-month training curriculum only in **2019** — 45 years after the ICDS program began.

10. Children with disabilities (CWSN) school enrollment **declined** from **1.1% (2013–14) to 0.9% (2018–19)** of total school enrollment — UDISE+ data showing the system is moving in the wrong direction despite progressive legislation.

11. **NSS 2018 data**: only **62.9% of persons with disabilities aged 3–35** have ever enrolled in any educational institution; only **23.1% are currently attending**.

12. A **1995 clinical study** showed that just **6 days of targeted training** enabled AWWs to identify disabilities with approximately **94% accuracy** — evidence that brief, competency-focused training can rapidly equip the existing workforce for disability identification.

13. **NCTE Draft Regulations 2025** contain **no explicit minimum qualification requirements for ECCE or NTT** (Nursery Teacher Training) — the regulatory gap for preschool teacher qualifications persists even in the most recent revision.

14. A **2025 Frontiers in Education study** (125 Delhi teachers) found that **principal and staff support** was the strongest single predictor of inclusive practice — stronger than individual teacher qualifications or demographics — challenging credential-focused policy approaches.

15. **Torquati et al. (2007)** meta-analysis confirms that ECCE teacher compensation is the strongest predictor of teacher stability, and stability is the strongest predictor of teacher-child interaction quality. International ECE turnover rates are **26–46% annually** in low-compensation settings.

16. **Barnett (2004)** quantified the compensation-quality relationship: each unit increase in ECCE teacher monthly pay (Rs 1,000 equivalent) is associated with approximately **0.15 standard deviations** improvement in classroom quality scores (CLASS tool).

$$\Delta Q \approx 0.15\sigma \text{ per Rs } 1000/\text{month salary increase}$$

17. AWWs are responsible for **8 distinct ICDS functions**, resulting in only **2–3 hours/day spent on ECCE activities** against a mandated 3.5 hours.

18. **40% of AWCs lack a dedicated building**, operating from community halls, rented rooms, or open spaces; **60%** lack running water. Less than **1% of Indian educational institutions** are disability-friendly.

19. The play materials budget per AWC is **Rs 500 per quarter** — approximately Rs 5.50 per child per quarter at a 1:30 ratio.

20. **PMC 8988775 (Frontiers in Education, 2025)** meta-analysis found that teacher beliefs about disability explain **34% of variance** in inclusive practice quality — more than qualification level (12%), training hours (18%), or experience (8%).

21. The **Ability Foundation AWW Disability Detection Protocol (2024)** Tamil Nadu pilot demonstrated a **40% increase in early disability identification** following a 5-day competency-based training — the strongest evidence to date for targeted AWW inclusion training.

22. **72.3% of households with a person with disability** are unaware of government disability support schemes, compounding the access barrier for children with disabilities in ECCE.

23. **RTE Act 2009** covers ages 6–14 only; its Section 11 ECCE directive for under-6 is non-binding. Critically, RTE still **cross-references the PWD Act 1995** (which was replaced by RPWD 2016) — an unresolved legal inconsistency.

24. **NEP 2020** mandates all ECCE workers to hold a **2-year professional ECCE diploma by 2030**, and calls for pay parity with primary teachers — but specifies no budget allocation or enforcement timeline for either target.

25. **Karnataka, Kerala, Tamil Nadu, and Andhra Pradesh** consistently outperform national ECCE quality indicators; state variation is stark — UP shows only **43.7%** pre-primary enrollment vs. **86.6%** in Karnataka.

> [!WARNING]
> **Common Misconception 1:** "NEP 2020 has fixed India's ECCE qualification problem." — NEP 2020 sets targets (2-year diploma by 2030, pay parity) but provides no budget, no enforcement mechanism, and no clear implementation roadmap. The targets are aspirational, not implemented.

> [!WARNING]
> **Common Misconception 2:** "Private preschools are better than Anganwadis for children with disabilities." — Budget private preschools (serving ~18% of enrollment) have WORSE teacher qualifications, higher child:teacher ratios, and NO regulatory inclusion requirements compared to government Anganwadis. Quality is highly stratified; the "private = better" assumption holds only for premium-fee schools.

> [!WARNING]
> **Common Misconception 3:** "RPWD 2016 guarantees inclusive preschool education for children with disabilities." — RPWD creates the right, but the enforcement mechanism for ages 3–6 is absent because preschool is not covered by RTE's enforcement architecture. The Vidhi Centre for Legal Policy identifies this as a jurisdictional vacuum requiring a RTE amendment.
