# Indian Philosophy — Key Facts

25 numbered facts for quick recall. Callouts highlight the most surprising. KaTeX for logical relationships. Warnings flag common misconceptions.

---

> [!NOTE]
> **Surprising Fact #1 — The World's Oldest Atomic Theory**
> Kanada proposed his atomic theory (Anu) around 600 BCE — centuries before Democritus in Greece, and over 2,400 years before Dalton's modern atomic theory (1803 CE). His Vaisheshika Sutras describe atoms as eternal, indivisible, and the building blocks of all matter.

1. **Indian philosophy spans 3,500 years** — from the composition of the Rig Veda (~1500 BCE) to Ramana Maharshi's death (1950 CE). This is longer than the entire span of Western philosophy from Socrates to today.

2. **The Vedas were transmitted orally for over a millennium before being written down.** Multiple recitation patterns (*pada-patha*, *krama-patha*, *jata-patha*) served as error-correction mechanisms — the equivalent of distributed checksums on an oral hard drive.

3. **There are 108 Upanishads**, of which 12–13 are considered "principal." The number 108 is considered sacred in Hindu tradition (12 × 9, or the ratio of the Sun's distance to Earth divided by the Sun's diameter).

> [!NOTE]
> **Surprising Fact #2 — Adi Shankaracharya lived only 32 years.**
> In 32 years (788–820 CE), Shankaracharya walked across India on foot (from Kerala to Kashmir to Assam to Gujarat), defeated every major philosopher of his age in debate, wrote commentaries on the Upanishads, Bhagavad Gita, and Brahma Sutras, composed devotional hymns (*stotras*), and established four monastic centres (*mathas*) that still function today. Most people accomplish less in 80 years.

4. **All three great Vedanta teachers came from South India.** Shankaracharya from Kerala, Ramanuja from Tamil Nadu, and Madhva from Karnataka — yet their philosophical debates shaped the theology of the entire Indian subcontinent.

5. **The Bhagavad Gita has been commented upon by more philosophers than any other text in the Indian tradition.** Shankara, Ramanuja, Madhva, Abhinavagupta, Jnaneshwar, Tilak, Gandhi, Aurobindo, Radhakrishnan — and hundreds of others — have each written major commentaries, often reaching contradictory conclusions from the same 700 verses.

6. **"Tat Tvam Asi" (That Thou Art) appears nine times in Chandogya Upanishad 6.** Uddalaka Aruni repeats it after each of nine different experiments (dissolving salt in water, rivers merging into the sea, etc.) to hammer the point home.

7. **The four Mahavakyas (Great Sayings) come one from each Veda:**
   - Rig Veda: *Prajnanam Brahma* (Consciousness is Brahman)
   - Sama Veda: *Tat Tvam Asi* (That Thou Art)
   - Yajur Veda: *Aham Brahmasmi* (I am Brahman)
   - Atharva Veda: *Ayam Atma Brahma* (This Self is Brahman)

> [!NOTE]
> **Surprising Fact #3 — The Bhagavad Gita is set on a battlefield, not in a meditation retreat.**
> The entire 700-verse philosophical masterwork is delivered in the minutes before a great war begins. Arjuna has his existential crisis — his weapons drop, he trembles — and Krishna's response is one of history's most comprehensive philosophical teachings. The dramatic urgency of the setting is deliberate: philosophy is not academic luxury; it must work under fire.

8. **Karma has three distinct components:**
   - *Sanchita* (accumulated past karma — the entire database)
   - *Prarabdha* (current-life karma — the running thread)
   - *Agami* (karma being generated now — new writes)

   As a formula: Total Karma = Sanchita + Prarabdha + Agami. Moksha = dissolving all three.

   $$K_{total} = K_{sanchita} + K_{prarabdha} + K_{agami}$$

   $$Moksha \Rightarrow K_{total} = 0$$

9. **Nagarjuna (2nd century CE) is considered by many historians the most influential philosopher in Asian history.** His Madhyamaka philosophy was the primary Buddhist response to Brahmanical metaphysics, and its logic directly influenced Shankara's Advaita — Shankara was sometimes called "a crypto-Buddhist" (*pracchanna bauddha*) by opponents.

10. **The Nyaya five-step syllogism is one of the world's first formal proof structures,** developed independently of Greek logic:
    1. *Pratijna* (thesis)
    2. *Hetu* (reason/evidence)
    3. *Udaharana* (example)
    4. *Upanaya* (application)
    5. *Nigamana* (conclusion)

    The Vyapti (invariable rule) connecting reason to conclusion is expressed as:

    $$\forall x: Smoke(x) \Rightarrow Fire(x)$$

    $$\text{(Wherever there is smoke, there is fire)}$$

11. **The three Gunas (Sattva, Rajas, Tamas) in Samkhya philosophy** describe the fundamental qualities of all matter and mind. Everything physical and mental is a combination of these three:
    - *Sattva* (clarity, light, harmony) → promotes knowledge and peace
    - *Rajas* (activity, passion, movement) → promotes action and desire
    - *Tamas* (inertia, darkness, obstruction) → promotes lethargy and delusion

> [!WARNING]
> **Misconception: "Maya means the world doesn't exist."**
> This is the most common misunderstanding of Advaita Vedanta. Shankara explicitly distinguishes between *asat* (non-existent, like a "sky-flower" — a flower in the sky that doesn't exist) and *mithya* (appears to have independent existence but doesn't). The world is *mithya* — not unreal, but not ultimately real either. A classic analogy: mistaking a rope for a snake in dim light. The snake is *mithya* — not nothing (you saw something real), but also not really a snake. The *rope* is the reality; the *snake* is the appearance. Brahman is the rope; the world is the snake.

12. **The Panchakosha (Five Sheaths) model of the self** from the Taittiriya Upanishad describes the human person as nested layers:
    1. Annamaya Kosha (physical body)
    2. Pranamaya Kosha (energy/breath body)
    3. Manomaya Kosha (mental body)
    4. Vijnanamaya Kosha (intellect body)
    5. Anandamaya Kosha (bliss body)
    Beyond all: Atman — the pure witnessing self.

> [!NOTE]
> **Surprising Fact #4 — Swami Vivekananda's 1893 Chicago Speech changed how the West saw India.**
> On September 11, 1893, Vivekananda addressed the Parliament of the World's Religions in Chicago. Beginning with "Sisters and Brothers of America" — which received a two-minute standing ovation — he presented Vedanta as a universal philosophy of religious tolerance. It was the first major public presentation of Indian philosophy to a Western audience and is considered a turning point in East-West intellectual dialogue.

13. **Ramanuja lived to approximately 120 years (1017–1137 CE)** — according to tradition, one of the longest-lived major philosophers in world history. He spent decades systematising Vishishtadvaita and organising the Sri Vaishnava community in Tamil Nadu.

14. **The Mandukya Upanishad (12 verses — the shortest principal Upanishad)** describes the four states of consciousness corresponding to the syllable AUM:
    - *Jagrat* (waking) = A
    - *Svapna* (dream) = U
    - *Sushupti* (deep sleep) = M
    - *Turiya* (the fourth — the witness beyond all three states) = the silence after AUM

15. **Patanjali's Yoga Sutras define yoga in one line:** *Yogaś citta-vṛtti-nirodhaḥ* — "Yoga is the cessation of the fluctuations of the mind-stuff." (Yoga Sutras 1.2). Everything else in the 196 sutras is commentary on this definition.

    $$Yoga = \text{Chitta-Vritti-Nirodha}$$
    $$= \text{(Mind-Stuff)(Fluctuations)(Cessation)}$$

> [!WARNING]
> **Misconception: "Karma is punishment."**
> Karma is not a cosmic justice system that punishes bad people. It is a neutral conservation law — like Newton's third law applied to intentional action. An action creates a consequence (not necessarily proportional, not necessarily immediate) that must be experienced. Karma is not moral retribution; it's more like technical debt — actions generate obligations that accumulate until resolved. And crucially: *actions performed without ego-attachment (Nishkama Karma) do not generate binding karma at all*.

16. **Buddhism rejects the Atman but not consciousness.** The Buddhist *Vijnanavada* school (Yogacara) held that only consciousness exists — sounds like Advaita! But the crucial difference: for Advaita, there is one universal consciousness (Brahman/Atman). For Vijnanavada, consciousness streams are momentary and relational — no permanent substrate. The difference is whether consciousness is a *permanent ground* or a *flowing process*.

17. **Madhvacharya (Dvaita) introduced a doctrine of predestination** — some souls are destined for eternal liberation (*nitya-muktas*), some for eternal suffering (*nitya-samsarins*), and some exist in a permanent middle state. This made Dvaita philosophically unique — and controversial — among Indian schools.

18. **The four paths to Moksha** in the Bhagavad Gita tradition are designed for different psychological types:
    - Jnana Yoga → for the intellectual
    - Bhakti Yoga → for the emotional
    - Karma Yoga → for the active
    - Raja Yoga → for the contemplative
    Most teachers say a person needs elements of all four, but one may dominate depending on temperament.

19. **The Bhakti Movement (8th–17th centuries CE) democratised Indian philosophy.** Poet-saints like Basavanna (Karnataka, Kannada), Andal (Tamil Nadu, Tamil), Mirabai (Rajasthan, Hindi), Tukaram (Maharashtra, Marathi), and Kabir (North India, Hindi/Urdu) wrote in vernacular languages — not Sanskrit — making spiritual teaching accessible to women, artisans, and people of all castes.

> [!NOTE]
> **Surprising Fact #5 — The Carvaka school argued that Vedic priests invented the afterlife to profit from rituals.**
> The Carvaka materialists were remarkably radical for their time. They argued: "Let the learned men perform the *agnihotra* rite and other rites? It is only a means of livelihood for the priests." Their materialism included rejecting Karma, rebirth, and God. They were India's ancient skeptics — and their critique, though we know it mostly through the refutations of their opponents, shows the tradition was always contested from within.

20. **Shankaracharya's four mathas (monastic centres) still function today** — over 1,200 years after their founding. The Sringeri Sharada Peetham in Karnataka is the oldest continuously operating philosophical institution in South India.

21. **The Brahma Sutras are so terse they're nearly unintelligible without a commentary.** Many sutras are just two or three words. Sutra 1.1.1: *"Athato Brahma jijnasa"* = "Now, therefore, the inquiry into Brahman." The word "atha" (now) alone generated dozens of pages of commentary debating *why* "now" — what preconditions make the student ready for this inquiry?

22. **"Neti neti" (not this, not this) is from the Brihadaranyaka Upanishad** — one of the earliest Upanishads (c. 700 BCE). The sage Yajnavalkya uses it to describe Brahman: it cannot be grasped by any quality, any description, any category. It is beyond all *this*. The method: systematically eliminate everything that consciousness is *not* until only the pure witness-consciousness remains.

23. **Ramanuja incorporated the Tamil devotional hymns of the Alvars** (12 poet-saints, 6th–9th centuries CE) into his theological framework. This was radical: Ramanuja elevated vernacular devotional poetry to the same status as Sanskrit scriptures — arguing that Tamil Prabandham and Sanskrit Vedas are co-equal in authority.

24. **The Bhagavad Gita chapter 11 — Vishwaroopa (Cosmic Form)** — is considered one of the most sublime passages in world literature. Arjuna sees the entire universe within Krishna, with infinite mouths devouring infinite forms. His reaction: *"I have seen what should never be seen. I am terrified. Be gracious, O God, and return to your gentle form."* The vision of God as cosmos is awesome in the original sense — awe and terror combined.

25. **Ramana Maharshi's entire teaching** — developed at Tiruvannamalai, Tamil Nadu — reduces to one practice: **"Who am I?"** (*Nan Yar?* in Tamil). Trace the sense of "I am" back to its source. Every thought arises from the "I-thought." Follow the "I-thought" to its root. What you find at the root is not a person — it is pure awareness. Ramana called this *Atma-Vichara* (Self-Inquiry) and considered it the most direct path to liberation.

> [!WARNING]
> **Misconception: "Indian philosophy is only about meditation and inner peace."**
> Indian philosophy includes atomic theory (Vaisheshika), formal logic (Nyaya), proto-evolutionary cosmology (Samkhya), cognitive science of perception (all schools' Pramana theories), philosophy of language (Mimamsa's *Sphotavada*), and political philosophy (*Arthashastra*, Mahabharata). The tradition is as analytically rigorous as any Western philosophical tradition — it's not all incense and silence.
