# Indian Philosophy — Glossary

~80 key terms, alphabetically ordered within categories. Every Sanskrit term is spelled out in English transliteration with its meaning, usage in context, and (where helpful) an engineering analogy.

---

## Category 1: Metaphysical Concepts

**Abhava** (अभाव)
: "Non-existence / absence." One of Kanada's seven categories (Padarthas) in Vaisheshika. Encompasses four types of absence: prior absence (before creation), posterior absence (after destruction), mutual absence (A is not B), and absolute absence (never exists). *Engineering analogy: null, empty set, and the absence of a key in a map.*

**Ahamkara** (अहंकार)
: Literally "I-maker." The ego principle — the faculty that creates the sense of "I am this particular person." In Samkhya, it arises from Mahat (cosmic intellect) and is the root of individual identity. Everything the ego touches generates karma. *Analogy: the process ID — the OS's way of tracking "this is process 4721," but the process itself doesn't know it's just an ID.*

**Anicca** (अनिच्च — Pali)
: "Impermanence." Buddhist doctrine that everything in conditioned existence changes moment to moment. One of the Three Marks of Existence. Nothing is permanent — not bodies, not thoughts, not feelings, not societies.

**Anatta** (अनत्ता — Pali) / Anatman (Sanskrit)
: "No-self." The Buddhist doctrine that there is no permanent, unchanging self (Atman). What we call "self" is a stream of interdependent mental and physical processes with no unchanging core. This directly contradicts the Upanishadic Atman doctrine.

**Anu** (अणु)
: "Atom." Kanada's term for the smallest, indivisible unit of matter. Anus are eternal, uncreated, and indestructible. Two Anus form a Dvyanuka (dyad). *The world's first atomic theory — predates Dalton by 2,500 years.*

**Antaryami** (अन्तर्यामी)
: "Inner Controller." In Ramanuja's Vishishtadvaita, Brahman/Vishnu is the Antaryami — the inner controller who dwells within and guides all souls and all matter from within. Every soul is simultaneously itself and God's instrument. *Analogy: the kernel process that underlies all user-space processes.*

**Atman** (आत्मन्)
: The individual self — the witnessing consciousness behind all thought, emotion, and perception. In Advaita, identical with Brahman. In Vishishtadvaita, a real but dependent entity. In Dvaita, real and eternally distinct from God. Not the body, not the mind — the unchanging witness behind them. *Analogy: the execution context, not the code running in it.*

**Bodham** (बोधम्)
: Pure awareness / enlightened knowing. From the root *budh* (to know, to awaken). The consciousness aspect of the self/Brahman — the knowing quality that makes experience possible. Related to Chit (the C in Sat-Chit-Ananda). *Analogy: the observer pattern at the OS level — not any particular program, but the capacity for programs to run and be observed.*

**Brahman** (ब्रह्मन्)
: The ultimate reality — the absolute ground of all existence. From root *brh* (to grow, to be vast). Described as Sat-Chit-Ananda (Existence-Consciousness-Bliss). Not a person, not a god with a form — more like the field of pure being that underlies everything. *Analogy: the runtime/OS that all processes depend on.*

**Chit** (चित्)
: Consciousness/Awareness. The second aspect of Brahman's nature in the Sat-Chit-Ananda formula. Brahman is not just *being* (Sat) but aware being. Chit is the self-luminous quality — like a lamp that illuminates itself while illuminating other things.

**Chitta** (चित्त)
: Mind-stuff / mental substance. In Yoga philosophy, Chitta is the medium in which all mental activity (thoughts, memories, emotions) occurs. Patanjali defines Yoga as "Chitta Vritti Nirodha" — the cessation of the fluctuations of Chitta.

**Dharma** (धर्म)
: Duty, right conduct, natural order, cosmic law. From root *dhri* (to hold, to sustain). Dharma is what "holds together" the universe and society. For an individual, Dharma is their particular duty — determined by stage of life (Ashrama), social role, and circumstances. One of the four **Purusharthas** (life-goals). *Analogy: the code contract — what a function is supposed to do, which it's obligated to honour.*

**Dravya** (द्रव्य)
: Substance. The first and most fundamental of Kanada's seven Padarthas. Vaisheshika identifies nine Dravyas: Earth, Water, Fire, Air, Space (Akasha), Time (Kala), Direction (Dik), Soul (Atman), and Mind (Manas). *Analogy: primitive data types.*

**Dvyanuka** (द्व्यणुक)
: A dyad — two atoms (Anus) combined. The first compound structure in Vaisheshika atomic theory. Dvyanukas form Tryanukas (triads) and eventually all gross matter.

**Guna** (गुण)
: Quality/attribute. In Vaisheshika, Gunas are the second Padartha — properties of substances. In Samkhya, there are three fundamental Gunas that compose all of Prakriti: Sattva (clarity/light), Rajas (activity/passion), and Tamas (inertia/darkness). Everything in the physical and mental world is a combination of these three.

**Jagat** (जगत्)
: The world / the universe — literally "that which moves / is ever-changing." In Advaita, Jagat is Mithya (appears real, ultimately unreal). In Vishishtadvaita and Dvaita, Jagat is fully real.

**Jiva** (जीव)
: The individual living being / embodied soul. In Vishishtadvaita and Dvaita, Jiva specifically refers to the individual soul encased in a body, subject to Samsara. In Advaita, Jiva is Atman + the limiting adjuncts (Upadhis) of body and mind.

**Jivanmukta** (जीवन्मुक्त)
: "Liberated while living." In Advaita Vedanta, a person who has realised Brahman-identity while still having a body. They continue to live out their Prarabdha karma (the karma already in motion) but generate no new binding karma and are inwardly free.

**Kaivalya** (कैवल्य)
: "Aloneness / isolation." The goal of Yoga (Patanjali) and Samkhya — the isolation of pure Purusha from entanglement with Prakriti. When Purusha "sees" that it was never actually bound by Prakriti, the apparent bondage dissolves.

**Mahat** (महत्)
: "The great one." In Samkhya cosmology, the first evolute of Prakriti — cosmic intellect. Also called Buddhi. From Mahat arises Ahamkara (ego), which divides into mind, senses, and elements.

**Maya** (माया)
: The cosmic power that makes Brahman appear as the multiplicity of the world. Not "illusion" in the sense of "unreal hallucination" — more like "that which appears to be what it is not." In Advaita, Maya has two functions: *Avarana* (veiling — hiding Brahman's true nature) and *Vikshepa* (projection — projecting the apparent world). *Analogy: the virtualisation layer — makes one underlying hardware appear as multiple separate machines.*

**Mithya** (मिथ्या)
: "Neither real nor unreal" — the status Advaita assigns to the phenomenal world. Not *asat* (non-existent) and not *sat* (absolutely real). The classic example: silver seen in a shell is *mithya* — not unreal (you saw something), but not really silver either.

**Moksha** (मोक्ष)
: Liberation / release from Samsara. The fourth and highest of the four Purusharthas (life goals). Defined differently by each school but universally agreed to be the cessation of the cycle of rebirth and the attainment of the highest possible state. *Analogy: the loop exits.*

**Prakriti** (प्रकृति)
: Nature / matter / the dynamic creative principle. In Samkhya, one of the two ultimate realities (paired with Purusha). Prakriti is unconscious but creative — it evolves the entire phenomenal world through 25 stages. Contains the three Gunas in equilibrium before creation begins.

**Purusha** (पुरुष)
: Pure consciousness / the witnessing self. In Samkhya, the second ultimate reality. Purusha is pure awareness — it doesn't act, doesn't change, doesn't create. It simply witnesses. Multiple Purushas exist (one per being). *Analogy: a read-only variable — it can be observed but not modified.*

**Saguna Brahman** (सगुण ब्रह्मन्)
: Brahman "with qualities" — God understood as having attributes (omniscience, omnipotence, compassion, etc.). The God you can pray to, relate to, and love. Ramanuja's and Madhva's God. The personal deity.

**Nirguna Brahman** (निर्गुण ब्रह्मन्)
: Brahman "without qualities" — the absolute beyond all attributes. You cannot say "God is loving" because Brahman transcends even love as a quality. Shankara's ultimate Brahman. Can only be pointed to by negation (*neti neti*).

**Sat** (सत्)
: Existence / Being / "that which is." The first aspect of Brahman's nature (Sat-Chit-Ananda). Sat never changes — it is pure, unchanging existence. Everything that exists does so because of Sat.

**Sunyata** (शून्यता)
: "Emptiness." The central concept of Nagarjuna's Madhyamaka Buddhism. Everything that exists is empty of inherent existence (*Svabhava*) — it exists only in dependence on other things. Even "emptiness" itself is empty of inherent existence (to avoid making Sunyata a new absolute).

**Upadhi** (उपाधि)
: "Limiting adjunct / imposed condition." In Advaita, the body-mind complex is an Upadhi that makes the infinite Atman appear finite and individual. Like a coloured glass that makes white light appear coloured — remove the glass (Upadhi) and only white light (Brahman) remains.

## Category 2: Epistemology (Theory of Knowledge)

**Anumana** (अनुमान)
: Inference. The second Pramana (valid knowledge source). Accepted by all schools. Involves reasoning from a known fact to an unknown fact via a universal rule (Vyapti). Classic example: "The hill has fire because it has smoke (wherever there is smoke, there is fire)."

**Anupalabdhi** (अनुपलब्धि)
: Non-perception / cognition of absence. The sixth Pramana, accepted by Mimamsa and Advaita. Knowing that a thing is absent by the very fact of not perceiving it when you should be able to. "There is no elephant in this room" — you know this by not seeing the elephant.

**Arthapatti** (अर्थापत्ति)
: Postulation / presumption / inference to the best explanation. Fifth Pramana. When two known facts can only be reconciled by assuming a third. "Devadatta is fat but doesn't eat in the day — therefore he must eat at night." *Analogy: abductive reasoning / hypothesis in debugging.*

**Pramana** (प्रमाण)
: Valid means / instrument of knowledge. The epistemological category for what counts as legitimate evidence. Each school defined its own list. *Analogy: your testing toolkit — what methods of verification you accept as proving correctness.*

**Pratyaksha** (प्रत्यक्ष)
: Direct perception. The first and most basic Pramana — accepted by all schools. Includes ordinary sensory perception and (for some schools) extraordinary non-sensory perception (yogic intuition, divine revelation).

**Sabda / Agama** (शब्द / आगम)
: Verbal testimony. Reliable spoken or written testimony from a trustworthy source. For Astika (orthodox) schools, this includes the Vedas as infallible testimony. *Analogy: trusted documentation — you don't reverify everything from scratch if the source is reliable.*

**Upamana** (उपमान)
: Comparison / analogy. Fourth Pramana, accepted by Nyaya and Mimamsa. Knowing a new thing by comparing it to a known thing. "A gavaya (wild ox) looks like a cow" — you know the gavaya when you see it in the forest because someone compared it to a cow.

**Vyapti** (व्याप्ति)
: Universal concomitance / invariable relation. The logical rule that connects reason (Hetu) to conclusion in inference. "Wherever there is smoke, there is fire" is a Vyapti. Must be established through observation across all cases and counter-checked against counter-examples. *Analogy: a verified logical invariant or precondition in formal verification.*

## Category 3: Philosophical Schools

**Advaita** (अद्वैत)
: "Non-dual." The school of Shankaracharya that holds Brahman as the only reality, Atman as identical with Brahman, and the world as Mithya (conventionally real, ultimately unreal). The most influential Vedanta sub-school.

**Astika** (आस्तिक)
: Orthodox — accepting the authority of the Vedas. The six Darshanas are all Astika. Does NOT simply mean "theistic."

**Carvaka** (चार्वाक)
: The Indian materialist school — accepts only direct perception (Pratyaksha) as valid knowledge, denies the soul, the afterlife, and karma. "Eat, drink, and be merry" is a caricature of its ethics, but it did argue that pleasure (Kama) is the highest good. Mostly known through critiques by other schools.

**Dvaita** (द्वैत)
: "Dualism." Madhvacharya's school — God (Vishnu), souls, and the world are eternally distinct. The most theistic of the three Vedanta sub-schools.

**Nastika** (नास्तिक)
: Heterodox — rejecting Vedic authority. Buddhism, Jainism, and Carvaka are Nastika. Does NOT mean "atheist" in the modern sense.

**Nyaya** (न्याय)
: "Logic / correct reasoning." The school of Gautama Muni focused on epistemology, logic, and debate theory. Developed the five-step syllogism (Panchavayava) and the 16 categories of logical discussion.

**Samkhya** (सांख्य)
: "Enumeration." The dualist school founded by Kapila that enumerates 25 categories (Tattvas) constituting all of existence, founded on the duality of Purusha (consciousness) and Prakriti (matter).

**Vaisheshika** (वैशेषिक)
: "Distinction / particularity." Kanada's atomic-ontological school that categorises all existence into seven Padarthas, with atoms (Anu) as the building blocks of matter.

**Vedanta** (वेदान्त)
: "End of the Vedas." The school based on the Upanishads, Brahma Sutras, and Bhagavad Gita. The most philosophically prominent of the six Darshanas, with three major sub-schools (Advaita, Vishishtadvaita, Dvaita).

**Vishishtadvaita** (विशिष्टाद्वैत)
: "Qualified non-dualism." Ramanuja's school — Brahman/Vishnu is the ultimate reality, but individual souls and the world are real and constitute God's "body." Neither purely non-dual nor purely dual.

## Category 4: Life Cycle and Ethics

**Agami Karma** (आगामी कर्म)
: Karma being generated right now by current intentional actions driven by ego. New entries being written to the karma ledger. *Analogy: new side-effects being generated in the currently running function.*

**Ahimsa** (अहिंसा)
: Non-violence / non-harm. One of the five Yamas (ethical restraints) in Patanjali's Yoga. Central to Jain ethics. Gandhi made it a political principle. *Considered the highest Dharma in most Indian traditions.*

**Artha** (अर्थ)
: Wealth / livelihood / prosperity. One of the four Purusharthas (life goals). Earning and managing material resources — important but subordinate to Dharma.

**Ashrama** (आश्रम)
: Stage of life. The four stages in the traditional Indian life model: Brahmacharya (student), Grihastha (householder), Vanaprastha (forest-dweller/semi-retirement), Sanyasa (complete renunciation). Each has its own Dharma.

**Bhakti** (भक्ति)
: Devotion / love of God. From root *bhaj* (to share, to participate). Bhakti is the path of emotional and loving relationship with the divine. Ramanuja placed it at the center of liberation. The Bhakti movement (8th–17th centuries) democratised spiritual practice across India.

**Dharma** (धर्म)
: (See Metaphysical Concepts above.) Also: one of the four Purusharthas — right conduct, duty, living according to one's nature and role.

**Janmam** (ജന്മം / जन्म)
: Birth / one iteration of Samsara. The Malayalam/Sanskrit word for the event of being born into a new body — one cycle of the Samsara loop. Determined by the type and quality of accumulated Prarabdha karma.

**Jnana** (ज्ञान)
: Knowledge / wisdom — specifically, direct experiential knowledge (not mere intellectual understanding). In Vedanta, Jnana is the direct realisation of Brahman-Atman identity. *The bug report that finally shows you the root cause, not just the symptoms.*

**Kama** (काम)
: Desire / pleasure / love. One of the four Purusharthas. Legitimate and important but to be pursued within the bounds of Dharma. The Kama Sutra is not just about sexuality — it's a treatise on the full art of refined living and pleasure.

**Karma** (कर्म)
: Action and its consequences. Every intentional action (driven by ego/desire) generates a seed that must bear fruit. *See also: Agami, Prarabdha, Sanchita Karma.*

**Moksha** (मोक्ष)
: Liberation. (See Metaphysical Concepts above.) The fourth and highest Purushartha.

**Nishkama Karma** (निष्काम कर्म)
: "Desireless action." Action performed without attachment to results. The core teaching of the Bhagavad Gita's Karma Yoga path. Act as your duty demands, offer the results to God, and do not be the anxious owner of outcomes. *Analogy: writing code for its correctness and elegance, not for the outcome metrics.*

**Prarabdha Karma** (प्रारब्ध कर्म)
: "Karma that has begun." The portion of accumulated karma (Sanchita) that is currently ripening — the karma generating your current lifetime's experiences. Cannot be avoided, must be lived through. *Analogy: the currently executing thread — you can't stop it mid-run, but you can influence how you respond to it.*

**Purushartha** (पुरुषार्थ)
: "The aims/goals of a person." The four life goals in Indian ethics: Dharma (right conduct), Artha (prosperity), Kama (pleasure), Moksha (liberation). The first three are conditional goods; Moksha is the unconditional highest good.

**Samsara** (संसार)
: The cycle of birth, death, and rebirth. Driven by karma. Ends with Moksha. *Analogy: an infinite loop with no exit condition as long as karma > 0.*

**Sanchita Karma** (संचित कर्म)
: The entire accumulated stock of karma from all past lives — the complete karma database that has not yet ripened. Most of it will never ripen in any single lifetime. *Analogy: the entire backlog of unprocessed tasks — most will never be executed.*

**Sanyasa** (संन्यास)
: Renunciation — the fourth Ashrama, when a person completely renounces worldly life, social roles, and possessions. Shankaracharya took Sanyasa at age 8 (by tradition) — exceptionally early, reflecting his extraordinary spiritual maturity.

**Viveka** (विवेक)
: Discrimination / discernment — the ability to distinguish between the real (Brahman/Atman) and the unreal (the world/body/mind complex). One of the four qualifications for Vedantic study, alongside Vairagya (dispassion), Shatsampat (six virtues), and Mumukshutva (burning desire for liberation). *Analogy: the ability to distinguish signal from noise in debugging.*

**Vairagya** (वैराग्य)
: Dispassion / detachment — not suppression of emotion but a natural cooling of the compulsive pursuit of transient pleasures when you've seen clearly that they don't deliver lasting satisfaction. Paired with Viveka.

## Category 5: Texts and Concepts

**Bhagavad Gita** (भगवद्गीता)
: "Song of God." 700-verse poem within the Mahabharata, in which Krishna teaches Arjuna on the battlefield. Synthesises Jnana, Karma, and Bhakti Yoga. Commented on by Shankara, Ramanuja, Madhva, and virtually every major Indian philosopher.

**Brahma Sutras** (ब्रह्मसूत्र)
: Also called Vedanta Sutras. 555 aphorisms by Badarayana (c. 400 BCE) systematising Upanishadic teaching. Part of the Prasthanatrayi (triple canon). Extremely terse — requires commentary to be understood.

**Mahavakya** (महावाक्य)
: "Great saying." Four key Upanishadic sentences: *Prajnanam Brahma* (Rig), *Aham Brahmasmi* (Yajur), *Tat Tvam Asi* (Sama), *Ayam Atma Brahma* (Atharva). Considered the complete distillation of Vedantic teaching.

**Neti Neti** (नेति नेति)
: "Not this, not this." Method of negation used to point to Brahman — systematically eliminating everything that Brahman is *not* (not the body, not the mind, not emotions, not the world) until only the pure witness remains. From Brihadaranyaka Upanishad 3.8.8.

**Prasthanatrayi** (प्रस्थानत्रयी)
: The "triple canon" of Vedanta: the Upanishads, Brahma Sutras, and Bhagavad Gita. Every Vedanta school must interpret all three consistently to be considered authoritative.

**Tat Tvam Asi** (तत् त्वम् असि)
: "That Thou Art." The most famous Mahavakya, from the Chandogya Upanishad 6.8.7. "That" (tat) = Brahman, the ultimate reality. "Thou" (tvam) = Atman, the individual self. The sentence asserts their identity (Advaita) or their intimate relation (Vishishtadvaita/Dvaita).

**Upanishad** (उपनिषद्)
: "Sitting near [a teacher]." The philosophical texts forming the concluding layer of the Vedas. 108 in total, 12–13 principal. Composed 800–200 BCE. The primary sources for all Vedanta philosophy.

**Veda** (वेद)
: "Knowledge." The four sacred texts: Rig, Sama, Yajur, Atharva. Held to be eternal and authoritative by all Astika (orthodox) schools. Transmitted orally with extraordinary precision for over a millennium before being written down.

**Yoga Sutras** (योग सूत्र)
: Patanjali's aphoristic text systematising yoga philosophy and practice. 196 sutras in four chapters (Samadhi, Practice, Psychic Powers, Liberation). Defines yoga as "Chitta Vritti Nirodha" (cessation of mental fluctuations).
